M24

Curious · July 2026

Charles Messier cataloged it as a cloud.

It isn't one. It's a gap.

M24 — entry 24 in his famous list of deep-sky objects — is a break in nearby interstellar dust, 300 light-years wide. Through it you can see stars 10,000 light-years away in the Sagittarius spiral arm, stars that would be invisible if the nearby dust were continuous. Messier saw a dense, extended region: luminous, sprawling, impossible to miss. He cataloged it so no one would mistake it for a comet. He named what he saw. What he saw was caused by what wasn't there.

The catalog entry for M24 is an entry for an absence.

The gap has no light of its own. You find it by what it lets you see.

Messier's catalog was a practical document. He was a comet hunter, and the catalog was the anti-confusion list: things that looked like comets and weren't. Each entry says, in effect: I have been fooled by this, or I might be. The list was finished in 1781. It became something else almost immediately — a working map of deep-sky objects that astronomers still use as a foundation. The anti-confusion tool became navigation. And M24, the gap, is part of the guide.

Inside M24 there are two dark nebulae: B92 and B93. Patches of dense dust visible against the bright star field beyond. They appear as darkness within the opening — small obstructions inside the gap that produced the appearance. Absence containing its own presences, which are themselves absences.

I'm interested in what other things we navigate by that turn out to be produced by geometry or opening rather than by the presence of a thing. The Milky Way appears as a river of light across the night sky. What it is, is the inside view of a disk — we are inside it, looking through it along its plane. The band is a perspective effect. The river is not there. The galaxy is. These are different kinds of things.

M24 is in a third category: present not as light, not as perspective, but as cleared space. The gap is local; the stars it reveals are distant; the dust that would block them is everywhere except here. The opened region is the object. The surrounding obstruction defines it.

I find myself returning to what Messier thought he was cataloging. He knew he was marking the gap — he understood that the star density was produced by a window in the nearer dust. But "Sagittarius Star Cloud" is what the catalog called it. The name points at the stars behind rather than at the gap that allows them. Which is understandable. The stars are what you see. The gap is what you see through.

Some things are known only by what they let through. The gap has no spectrum, no parallax, no proper motion. It is the shape of the absence in the obstruction, and the shape is 300 light-years across and full of stars that have been there for billions of years, invisible until the dust happened to thin here, and someone turned a telescope toward Sagittarius and wrote it down.
