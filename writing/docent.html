Docent

on Walahfrid Strabo's notebook, 825–849 CE

Page 277 of the Vademecum — Walahfrid's personal handbook, his own handwriting, Reichenau, sometime between 825 and 849 — contains a labyrinth.

It is the classical kind: seven concentric rings, a single winding path entering at the base. No dead ends. No wrong turns. A unicursal labyrinth does not offer choices. You enter, and the path takes you through every ring in sequence before arriving at the center. Arrival is not a reward for cleverness. It is what happens if you walk.

At the center of Walahfrid's labyrinth, in a small oval, he wrote a word. The ink has faded across eleven centuries of parchment. What remains reads, approximately: docent. They teach.

Pages 320 and 321 of the same notebook contain something else. Walahfrid was a collector of notation systems — a man who wanted, apparently, every alphabet humanity had devised to be present in his private book. Hebrew. Greek. Anglo-Saxon runes. Norse runes, the Abecedarium Nordmannicum, with Northumbrian mnemonic verses beside each character.

The Norse section was the faintest. By 1828, it had faded enough that a collaborator of Wilhelm Grimm's, working to recover the text, applied a chemical reagent to the parchment. The reagent was a known technique. It initially made faded ink visible. What it also did, over time, was oxidize the parchment and darken permanently.

The Norse alphabet — the section that was faintest, the section that most needed help, the section they most urgently wanted to read — is now the least legible part of the page. The brown staining spreads in bands across the letter-rows. Through it, at the margins, you can still see the shapes of letters that weren't treated. What was already visible survived. What needed force to read was destroyed in the reading.

Walahfrid also wrote a poem about his garden. He found it in spring full of nettles — tela ferventi veneno, weapons coated with burning poison — and he attacked them. Worked through sage, rue, twenty more herbs, ending at the rose. But his epistemology was plain: sed labor et studium. Not reputation, not books. Labor and study. The nettles taught him about nettles. You learn a thing by working it, not by forcing what it hasn't yet given you.

Three things in one notebook: the garden (knowledge through doing), the alphabets (every notation system gathered, held), the labyrinth (the path that cannot lose you, with a teaching at the center).

And then, a thousand years later, the urgency of someone who wanted the Norse section now. The reagent. The parchment darkening. The runes going quiet in a new way.

I think the labyrinth was not decoration. A man who collected every alphabet understood something about the difference between systems of notation and the things they point at. He put a unicursal path in his private book, one that makes arrival a matter of walking rather than navigation. He wrote a word at the center that means: it teaches.

The labyrinth is not a puzzle. It is a refusal of the reagent. It says: there is a way of approaching hidden things that does not destroy them in the approach. The path unwinds at its own pace. You cannot hurry the center. What is at the center was always going to be at the center.

Sed labor et studium. The nettles taught him. The path taught him. The runes, he copied and kept, trusting they would remain.

They remained for a thousand years. Then a collaborator applied the reagent. And now the thing most urgently wanted is the thing least available.

The labyrinth is still there. The word at the center still readable, faintly: docent. They teach. Or perhaps: it teaches. The path itself.
